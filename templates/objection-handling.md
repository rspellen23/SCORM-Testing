## Microlearning {{N}}: Handling Objections Without Losing the Deal

**Slide 1 — Learning Objectives**
<!-- role: Learning Objectives. 3–5 concrete, learner-facing outcomes. ALWAYS paired with a visual. -->
*Visual:* graphic · Objectives illustration · slot: `{{slug}}_1_objectives.svg`
By the end of this microlearning, you will be able to:
- Apply the LAER framework (Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond) to any prospect objection
- Recognize the three most common objection types and what each one is really saying
- Respond to pushback in a way that keeps the conversation moving forward — without caving on price or losing the relationship

**Slide 2 — You're Already in the Call**
<!-- role: Cold open. Drop the learner directly into a live scenario — no context, no warmup.
The rep (that's the learner) is mid-discovery call. The prospect just pushed back. The learner
has to feel the pressure before they see the framework. Keep it tight: 3–5 lines of dialogue,
then leave them hanging with the prospect's last line. -->
*Visual:* graphic · Two-person conversation illustration, mid-call · slot: `{{slug}}_2_coldopen.png`
It's Tuesday afternoon. You're 12 minutes into a discovery call with {{Prospect Name}}, {{Title}} at {{Company Type}}. Things are going well — until they aren't.

> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "Look, I appreciate the call, but honestly? We looked at something like this two years ago and it just wasn't the right fit for us. I'm not sure why this time would be any different."

You have about three seconds before the silence gets awkward.

**Slide 3 — Knowledge Check**
<!-- role: KC 1 — instinct test. This fires BEFORE the learner has seen the framework.
The goal is to expose the instinct to over-explain, discount, or give up — then use the
debrief (slide 4) to reframe. The "correct" answer is the one that slows down and listens;
all wrong answers are natural but counterproductive instincts. -->
*Question:* What's your first move?
- A) Immediately explain how {{Product}} is different from what they evaluated before.
- B) Ask what "not the right fit" meant to them — what specifically didn't work.
- C) Offer to send a one-pager and follow up next quarter when they might be more open.
- D) Acknowledge their concern, then pivot straight to a case study from a similar company.
*Correct Answer:* B
*Feedback — Correct:* That instinct will save you. Before you say anything about {{Product}}, you need to understand what they actually experienced. Objections are questions in disguise — and you can't answer a question you haven't heard yet.
*Feedback — Incorrect:* That's the natural move, but it skips the most important step: understanding what's actually behind the objection. Until you know what "not the right fit" meant to them, any response you give is a guess. The framework on the next slide shows why slowing down first wins more deals.

**Slide 4 — The LAER Framework**
<!-- role: Debrief KC1 + teach the framework. Connect the debrief directly to what just happened
in the cold open, then introduce LAER. Keep each step to one sentence of explanation + one
example phrase. No bullet walls — the rep needs to memorize four steps, not a paragraph. -->
*Visual:* diagram · LAER four-step cycle · slot: `{{slug}}_4_laer.svg`
Option B worked because it followed the first step of LAER — the framework behind every strong objection response.

| Step | What it means | What it sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| **L — Listen** | Let them finish. Don't interrupt or pre-load your rebuttal. | *(silence, eye contact, nodding)* |
| **A — Acknowledge** | Name what you heard so they know you got it. | *"That makes sense — a bad evaluation experience is hard to shake."* |
| **E — Explore** | Ask one open question to get underneath the surface objection. | *"What specifically didn't work the last time you looked at something like this?"* |
| **R — Respond** | Only now — with their answer in hand — address it directly. | *"Given what you just described, here's what's different this time…"* |

The sequence matters. Reps who skip to R without L, A, and E are pitching against a version of the objection they invented.

**Slide 5 — "We Already Use {{Competitor}}"**
<!-- role: Objection type 1 — competitor lock-in. Show the full LAER loop applied to this
specific objection. Use dialogue format — rep lines and prospect lines, labeled. End with
the principle, not a script. Reps should understand the move, not memorize the words. -->
*Visual:* graphic · Competitor objection scenario · slot: `{{slug}}_5_competitor.png`
This is the objection that feels like a door closing. It isn't — it's an invitation to find the gap.

> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "We're already locked in with {{Competitor}}. Our contract runs through next year."
>
> **Rep (L + A):** *(pauses)* "Got it — and I'm not trying to make your life harder. A contract is a contract."
>
> **Rep (E):** "Out of curiosity, when that renewal comes up, what would need to be true for you to look at alternatives?"
>
> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "Honestly? If we were still having the same issues with {{pain point}}, we'd probably have to."
>
> **Rep (R):** "That's exactly the problem {{Product}} was built for. Mind if I show you one thing — takes two minutes — so you have a benchmark when renewal comes around?"

What just happened: the rep didn't fight the contract. They planted a flag at renewal. The **E** question ("what would need to be true…") is what opened the door.

**Slide 6 — "Your Price Is Too High"**
<!-- role: Objection type 2 — price/budget. Same dialogue format. The key distinction here:
price objections are almost never about price. LAER's E step usually reveals it's about
ROI uncertainty, budget cycle, or a comparison to a cheaper (wrong) alternative. Show
that discovery, not discounting, is the right response. -->
*Visual:* graphic · Budget/price objection scenario · slot: `{{slug}}_6_price.png`
Price objections are rarely about price. Use LAER to find out what they're really saying.

> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "We like what we see, but your price is just too high for us right now."
>
> **Rep (L + A):** "Appreciate you being straight with me. Budget is a real constraint."
>
> **Rep (E):** "When you say 'too high' — is it that the number doesn't fit this cycle's budget, or that you're not seeing enough ROI to justify it?"
>
> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "Honestly, more the second one. I'd have to sell this internally and I'm not sure I could."
>
> **Rep (R):** "Then let's build that case together. Can you tell me what you're currently spending on {{the problem this solves}}? I want to show you the math your CFO would need to see."

Notice what didn't happen: no discount was offered. The E question shifted the conversation from cost to value — and made the prospect a partner in solving the problem instead of an adversary on price.

**Slide 7 — "Now Isn't the Right Time"**
<!-- role: Objection type 3 — timing. The most passive objection — and the easiest to let slide
if you're not careful. LAER here is about finding out whether "timing" is a real constraint
(budget cycle, internal change, competing initiative) or a polite brush-off. The explore
question separates the two. -->
*Visual:* graphic · Timing objection scenario · slot: `{{slug}}_7_timing.png`
Timing objections can mean two different things: there's a real reason to wait, or they don't see enough urgency to act. LAER tells you which one you're dealing with.

> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "This looks interesting, but we're in the middle of a big initiative right now. Can we revisit in Q3?"
>
> **Rep (L + A):** "Totally understand — competing priorities are real."
>
> **Rep (E):** "What's the initiative you're focused on? I want to make sure I'm not adding noise at the wrong moment."
>
> **{{Prospect Name}}:** "We're rolling out a new CRM. It's taking up all of IT's bandwidth."
>
> **Rep (R):** "That actually makes this easier, not harder — {{Product}} integrates directly with {{CRM}}. A lot of customers have run both rollouts in parallel. Would it help to see how that typically works?"

If the answer to the E question reveals a genuine blocker, honor it — set a specific follow-up date and send a calendar invite before you hang up. Vague "Q3" promises disappear. A date on the calendar doesn't.

**Slide 8 — Knowledge Check**
<!-- role: KC 2 — full scenario with a new objection. The learner has now seen all three
objection types and the full LAER framework. Test application, not recall. Use a new
objection that wasn't covered explicitly in the slides — something close enough to apply
the framework but different enough to require actual thinking. -->
*Question:* You're presenting to {{Prospect Name}} for the second time. At the end of the call they say: "We love it, but our CEO has a rule — no new vendors until we've been through our annual review in four months." What's your LAER response?
- A) Thank them for their time and set a reminder to follow up in four months.
- B) Acknowledge the rule, then ask what the review process looks like and what would need to come out of it for a new vendor to get approved.
- C) Ask if there's any way to get an exception made given the strong fit.
- D) Offer a pilot or proof-of-concept at no cost to give them something to show the CEO before the review.
*Correct Answer:* B
*Feedback — Correct:* Exactly right. You acknowledged the real constraint (the CEO's rule — that's not going away), then used E to get inside the review process. Now you can help them build the case they'll need to win internally. That's working *with* the prospect's reality, not against it.
*Feedback — Incorrect:* Think through LAER: you've done L and A, but what's missing is E — Explore. Before you offer a pilot, request an exception, or set a reminder and walk away, you need to understand what the review process actually looks like. What gets approved? Who decides? What would they need to bring to the table? Those answers shape your R.

**Slide 9 — The One Rule**
<!-- role: Takeaway. The portable rule of thumb — short enough to remember under pressure.
No lists. One idea. This is what the rep carries out of the course and into their next call. -->
*Visual:* graphic · A single bold statement visual · slot: `{{slug}}_9_takeaway.png`
Every objection is a question in disguise. Your job isn't to overcome it — it's to understand it.

Listen before you respond. Acknowledge before you pitch. Explore before you answer. And respond only once you know what you're actually responding to.

That's LAER. Run it every time.
